I believe in the power of visual communication. Instead of telling you what I do ... I'll show you.

The first step in problem solving is deciding what you want from your project. If you're not sure what the goal is, you can't know if the project is successful.

The next step is to generate material. Some of it turns out well and much of it fails. The trick is to create as much as possible to create a pool of ideas to draw from later.

Once you have your set of ideas it then becomes the task to use those in conjunction with the goals to create something meaningful and clear.

The last step is to share that work with the world. This could be through traditional print media, or through online sources. It's often at this point we also evaluate the end product against the initial goals. Is there more work to be done?

Classic Nursery Rhymes

Problem: Translating a nursery rhyme in one image

This was an illustration project from Word & Image II during the fall of Junior year. We were given a list of nursery rhymes to recreate. This was an exploration of character, motion, and narrative.

These three images illustrate Little Nancy Etticoat, Simple Simon Met a Pieman, and Pussy Cat Pussy Cat Where Have You Been?

Little Nancy Etticoat is about a melting candle. I was drawn to the quiet moment of realization of her fate. Simple Simon provided a rediculous interaction with a huge pieman who keep his pies from being ruined. Finally, Pussy Cat Pussy Cat pushed me to explore animalistic expression and contortion.

Bad Day for a Waitress

Problem: Creating Movement

Word & Image I final during the spring of Sophomore year. I was assigned "Bad Day for a Waitress" as a topic to explore character. The challenge was in creating a narrative character in action.

First we researched how others have solved this problem of character, space, and narrative. We looked at the works of Lowell Hess, Rea Irvin, and Harry Beckhoff in early to mid 20th century in a visit to WashU's West Campus.

Armed with some reference knowledge, I began skeching. I gravitated towards the idea of a waitress who has too much to carry. Her struggled posture is dynamic and engaging. Her surroundings melt away as her attention is focused on not dropping this mass of plates and dishes she has to carry.

We were allowed to use black and one other color to describe our character. I used the most saturated version of my red in the legs and hair to draw attention to those key points.

Collections Poster: Gaze

Problem: Creating a poster with a collection of self-generated images

Word & Image I project during the spring of Sophomore year. We were each given a topic from which to generate a collection of 8 to 20 items. I explored the collection of "Stargazing and the Constellations." First I was interested in exploring cross cultural and cross temporal views of the night sky. I was attached to different types of windows. After weeks of this approach focused more on the windows and the culture rather than the sky, I had to change my strategy

When I found myself stuck starting over I grabbed my india ink and some brushes. I made some textures and strokes and scanned them at a very high resolution. I combined the effects I got from the ink in photoshop to get night sky characteristics. All of the items I represented were visible in the night sky without any equipment.

There is a connection between the lack of technology required in seeing these phenomena and the use of the physical texture of the ink media.

The collection is arranged in a verticle arrangement. The galaxy is the isolated on the left both because it was a more detailed exploration and also because of it's importance. It was challenging to create a way to add a caption to an isolated image but I found a way to connect the two in calling out the (Left) position.

This poster aims to educate the viewer on what sorts of things they can see in the nightsky without requiring any expensive technology.

Fema Handbook

Problem: Clarity & Function

A junior project in Type involved re-laying out a FEMA handbook about disaster preparedness. The old version did not clearly engage the viewer and the flow of the text was not clear. Our critical restriction was the final would be displayed in a three ring binder.

We had to carefully develop a grid system and paragraph styles. We had to create a grid which was both engaging, but also functional.

I choose a landscape view and a three column grid. When the user needed space to write I often opened up two of those columns for writing space. The challenge with this view is that the flow could get tricky. Using headers and flow lines I keep the user moving through each page.I included checkboxes and lists for the user to easily interact with the information.
